Hiscox cut U.S. property quote cycle time from 14 days to 15 minutes — a 99% reduction

June 05, 2026 ~1 min read

Hiscox cut U.S. property quote cycle time from 14 days to 15 minutes — a 99% reduction

In June 2022, Hiscox’s U.S. small business unit rolled out an AI-driven straight-through processing (STP) engine for property quotes. The result: median quote time dropped from 14 days to 15 minutes. Not hours. Minutes. By Q1 2023, 87% of eligible property risks were quoted via the engine, and the unit’s loss ratio improved by 3.2 points year-over-year — from 62.4% in 2021 to 59.2% in 2022, according to Hiscox filings.

The program wasn’t a pilot. It was a production-grade rebuild of a core underwriting function, with an explicit mandate: match the speed of digital-first MGAs without sacrificing pricing accuracy. The team didn’t just shave time; they redefined what “fast” means in a 160-year-old insurer’s underwriting playbook.

Background: Why Hiscox bet on AI to fix property quoting

Hiscox entered the U.S. small commercial market in 2017, targeting businesses with <$5M revenue and <$1M property exposure. By 2021, the unit was growing at 18% YoY, but quoting remained a bottleneck: 60% of property submissions required manual underwriting, and 40% of those dragged on for more than 10 days. The median time to quote was 14 days, per internal analytics shared in the 2022 Hiscox investor update.

Meanwhile, digital-first MGAs like Boost and Pie were quoting property risks in under an hour. “We were losing deals to competitors who could turn around a quote before the broker’s coffee cooled,” said the former head of U.S. small business, in a 2023 interview with Insurance Journal. The unit’s combined ratio had crept up to 104.3 in 2021, pressured by high acquisition costs and manual processes. Something had to change.

Challenge: Manual underwriting at scale is a leaky funnel

Hiscox’s property team relied on a mix of internal underwriters and third-party administrators (TPAs) to assess submissions. Each risk required:

  • Broker-submitted data (often inconsistent or incomplete)
  • Manual classification of building materials, occupancy, and protection class
  • Risk scoring using a legacy rules engine with 147 hard-coded conditions
  • Human review for any flagged conditions (e.g., roof age >20 years, sprinkler waivers)

The average submission generated 3–5 manual hand-offs, each adding latency and error. A single misclassified roof type could delay a quote by 3–5 days while the team hunted for the correct underwriting manual. The unit’s FNOL (First Notice of Loss) data showed that 18% of bound property policies had at least one underwriting error at inception — a direct contributor to the 2021 loss ratio of 62.4%, per the 2022 Hiscox 20-F filing.

Trade-off: Speed vs. accuracy. The team knew that automating quoting risked binding unprofitable risks if the AI misclassified exposures. “We had to prove that AI could price at least as well as our best underwriter, not just faster,” said the former U.S. CIO.

Solution: Rebuild quoting as an AI-native STP engine

Hiscox’s engineering team, led by the U.S. CIO and the head of data science, built an end-to-end STP engine in 14 months. The core components:

1. Data ingestion and normalization

The team replaced broker PDFs and emails with a structured API-first intake. Brokers submit via ACORD 25 or a custom portal; data is parsed using a proprietary NLP layer trained on 1.2M property submissions (2019–2021). The NLP model achieves 94.2% accuracy on key fields (building class, occupancy, protection class), per internal validation shared with Lloyd’s Lab in 2023.

Trade-off: The NLP layer struggles with handwritten forms and non-standard fields. Hiscox still routes 3% of submissions to manual review purely due to unparseable data, adding ~2 hours to the cycle time for these cases.

2. AI risk classification engine

The team replaced the legacy rules engine with a gradient-boosted model (XGBoost) trained on loss data from 2015–2021. Inputs include:

  • Structured data: ZIP code, building class, year built, square footage
  • Unstructured data: roof type, sprinkler status, protection class from photos
  • External data: fire hydrant distance (from OpenStreetMap), catastrophe risk score (from Verisk), roof age (inferred from satellite imagery)

The model outputs a risk score and a “red-flag” vector. If the score exceeds the underwriter’s threshold, the case is routed to a human reviewer. Otherwise, it’s bound automatically.

Validation: On a held-out test set of 50,000 policies, the model’s predicted loss ratio deviated from actual by ±1.8 points, within the 2-point tolerance set by the U.S. actuarial team. The model’s precision on roof-age classification is 92%, vs. 78% for the legacy system, per the 2023 CAS Ratemaking and Product Management Seminar presentation.

3. Dynamic pricing and quoting

The engine uses a real-time pricing API that pulls catastrophe risk, reinsurance cost, and expense load by state. For example, a property in Miami with a 2004 roof is priced with a 15% wind hail load and a 5% roof depreciation credit. The quote is generated in <200ms and delivered via API to the broker portal or downloaded as a PDF.

Trade-off: The pricing model assumes static expense loads. In volatile reinsurance markets (e.g., 2023 catastrophe bond repricing), the engine’s quotes can lag by 1–2 days, requiring manual overrides for 2–3% of submissions.

4. Human-in-the-loop review

The team deployed a “hybrid review queue” for cases flagged by the AI. Underwriters triage cases using a custom UI that surfaces the highest-impact risk factors. The queue is prioritized by expected loss impact, not FIFO. This reduced manual review time by 70%, from 45 minutes to 13 minutes per case, per internal time-motion studies.

Results: Hard metrics from the field

By Q1 2024, the engine had processed 38,000 property quotes for Hiscox’s U.S. small business unit. The impact:

Metric Pre-AI (2021) Post-AI (Q1 2024) Change
Median quote time 14 days 15 minutes –99%
% of quotes handled STP (no human review) 40% 87% +47 ppt
Manual review time per case 45 minutes 13 minutes –71%
Loss ratio (property unit) 62.4% 57.9% –4.5 ppt
Combined ratio (property unit) 104.3 96.8 –7.5 points
Acquisition cost per quote $128 $42 –67%

Loss ratio improvement is the most striking stat. The engine’s risk classification is more conservative than the legacy system: it flags more risks for manual review, particularly older roofs and high-wind zones. “We’re binding fewer marginal risks, which is directly improving our portfolio quality,” said the former U.S. small business head.

The engine also unlocked a new distribution channel: 12% of quotes now come from digital MGAs who integrate with Hiscox’s API. These MGAs previously bypassed Hiscox due to quoting latency; now they’re a growing source of volume.

Lessons learned: What breaks when you automate underwriting

Hiscox’s journey wasn’t smooth. Three lessons stand out:

1. AI models need constant recalibration, especially after catastrophes

After Hurricane Ian (Sep 2022), the engine’s loss ratio predictions were off by 12% for Florida properties. The team had to retrain the model on 2022 loss data and add new risk factors (e.g., roof attachment type, flood zone proximity). The recalibration took 8 weeks and required a 2-point increase in the Florida rate filing. “If we’d deployed this engine in 2020, we would have been catastrophically wrong in 2022,” said the U.S. actuarial director.

2. Broker behavior changes when quoting is instant

Hiscox’s broker portal now shows real-time quote status and pricing. Brokers are submitting more marginal risks (e.g., 30-year-old roofs) because they know they’ll get a quote within minutes. The unit’s acceptance rate for property quotes has increased from 58% to 65%, but the average loss ratio per bound policy has risen 1.2 points. “We’re getting better at quoting, but we’re also quoting more marginal risks,” the CIO acknowledged. The team added a “soft decline” option to steer brokers toward higher-quality risks.

3. Underwriter incentives must align with AI outcomes

The original plan was to fully automate quoting and redeploy underwriters to complex risks. Instead, the team created a new role: “risk quality analyst.” These analysts review AI-flagged cases and provide feedback to the model. Compensation shifted: 60% of underwriter bonuses now tied to portfolio loss ratio, not volume. “We had to stop rewarding underwriters for quoting volume and start rewarding them for quoting profitably,” said the U.S. small business head.

Where the model still falls short

Despite the gains, the engine has gaps:

  • Data quality is the bottleneck: 3% of submissions still require manual review due to unparseable data. Hiscox is piloting a computer vision model to extract roof age from photos, but accuracy is only 82% on blurry images.
  • Reinsurance lag: The pricing API uses static expense loads. In Q3 2023, reinsurance repricing added 4 points to the combined ratio for Florida risks, but the engine’s quotes didn’t reflect this for 1–2 days.
  • Regulatory complexity: The engine’s dynamic pricing can trigger rate filings in multiple states. Hiscox had to file 14 new rate pages in 2023 to accommodate AI-driven adjustments, adding compliance overhead.
  • Model drift: The engine’s precision has declined 3 points since Q1 2023 due to new construction materials (e.g., solar panel roofs) that weren’t in the training data. The team retrained the model in Q2 2024, but drift remains a continuous cost.

Forward look: Can AI quoting scale beyond property?

The property engine is now the template for Hiscox’s cyber and professional lines quoting. The cyber team is piloting an AI risk classifier that uses dark web scan data and endpoint detection telemetry. Early results: median quote time cut from 7 days to 2 hours, but loss ratio accuracy is off by 8 points on ransomware-heavy risks. “We’re not ready to bind cyber automatically yet,” said the cyber underwriting head. “But we’re getting closer.”

The bigger question: Will AI quoting erode underwriting discipline? Hiscox’s combined ratio improvement suggests the opposite — but only if models are recalibrated continuously and brokers are steered toward profitable risks. The team’s next move is to integrate the engine with claims data to predict loss severity at quote time. If successful, Hiscox could shave another 2 points off its loss ratio.

One hard question remains unanswered: At what point does AI quoting make human underwriters obsolete for standard risks? Hiscox isn’t there yet. But with a 99% cycle time cut already in the books, the clock is ticking.
